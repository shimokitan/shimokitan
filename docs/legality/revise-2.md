## The Copyright Situation: Your Real Exposure

### What You're Doing Right

**The "No-Host" Defense** is smart - you're not uploading copyrighted video/audio files. But here's the problem:

### Where You're Vulnerable

**1. Thumbnails Are NOT Safe Just Because You Say So**

Your policy says:
> "Thumbnails and cover images... are used in a referential, indexing capacity similar to how search engines display thumbnail previews."

**Legal reality**: Search engines have:
- Massive legal teams and settlements
- Transformative use arguments (helping users find content)
- Implied licenses from sites they index
- Deep pockets to defend fair use

You have none of these. **A thumbnail is still a copyrighted image.** The "search engine" analogy doesn't automatically protect you - courts evaluate this case-by-case.

**2. Your Actual Use Case is Murkier Than Search**

Search engines show thumbnails to help users **find the original source**. You're showing thumbnails to:
- Curate/editorialize ("Shimokita Score")
- Build your own aesthetic/brand
- Keep users on YOUR site engaging with YOUR curation

This is **less transformative** than search. You're adding value, but you're also potentially substituting for official fan wikis, databases, or promotional materials.

**3. "Sourced from their respective platforms" Doesn't Grant You Rights**

Just because YouTube has a thumbnail doesn't mean:
- You can use it elsewhere
- YouTube gave you permission (their ToS likely prohibits scraping)
- The original rights holder (anime studio, record label) approves

**4. The "Archival Index" Label Isn't a Legal Shield**

"Archival" suggests preservation under specific copyright exceptions (libraries, cultural institutions). You're a **commercial venture** (even with affiliate links as future revenue) doing editorial curation. That's not archival in the legal sense.

### What's Actually Happening

You're relying on:
1. **Fair use** (US concept, doesn't exist in Indonesia)
2. **Being too small to sue** (true until you're not)
3. **Rights holders not caring** (they might, especially Japanese studios/labels who are notoriously protective)

### The Indonesia Problem

Indonesian copyright law (UU Hak Cipta) is **stricter** than US fair use:
- No broad fair use exception
- Limited exceptions for criticism/review (and that's debatable for thumbnails)
- Criminal penalties possible
- International enforcement via treaties (Berne Convention, TRIPS)

**You're governed by Indonesian law but using US services and Japanese content.** This is a jurisdictional nightmare if someone comes after you.

### What You Need to Fix

**Immediate (Before Launch):**

1. **Get Real About Risk**
Add a section like:
> "**Rights Holder Notice**: Shimokitan operates under the good-faith belief that our use of thumbnail images constitutes fair dealing for purposes of criticism, review, and cultural documentation. We do not host original media files. If you are a rights holder with concerns about specific content, please contact us at registry@shimokitan.live and we will respond promptly."

2. **Tighten Your Removal Process**
Your current policy says "10 business days" - that's too long. Major platforms respond in 24-48 hours. Make it "within 48 hours for simple requests, up to 5 business days for complex cases."

3. **Add a Limitation**
State clearly:
> "Shimokitan does not provide legal advice regarding fair use or copyright status of indexed content. Each visitor is responsible for their own use of linked content."

4. **Consider Watermarking/Attribution**
For every thumbnail, include visible attribution (© Studio Name, sourced via YouTube). Makes takedown requests easier and shows good faith.

**Strategic (Next 3 Months):**

5. **Get Permission Where Possible**
Contact smaller studios, indie labels, creators. Say "We'd love to feature your work - can we use promotional images?" Many will say yes for free publicity.

6. **Build a Whitelist System**
Track which rights holders have:
- Given explicit permission
- Issued takedowns
- Never responded

Prioritize featuring content from Column 1.

7. **Consider Partnerships**
Reach out to Crunchyroll, Funimation, etc. for official partnerships where you'd use THEIR licensed thumbnails with permission in exchange for affiliate revenue share.

8. **Get Legal Insurance**
Seriously. "Errors & Omissions" insurance for media/tech ventures. ~$500-2000/year. Covers legal defense costs.

**Nuclear Option (If You Want to Sleep at Night):**

9. **Switch to Text-Only with Official Links**
Instead of thumbnails, use:
- Title + description text
- "View on [Platform]" links
- Official platform embeds only (YouTube player, not just thumbnail)

Way less compelling visually, but legally bulletproof.

### The Brutal Truth

**Your current policy protects you legally: maybe 30-40%.**

It's well-written for what it is, but it's based on assumptions ("like search engines," "archival index") that don't hold up under scrutiny. You're hoping:
- Small size = beneath notice
- Goodwill = no one sues
- Takedown compliance = enough

**That works until it doesn't.** One angry studio, one viral moment, one rights holder having a bad day = legal threat.

### My Recommendation

**You can launch with this**, but within 90 days you need to either:

A) **Get legal counsel** in Indonesia who specializes in digital media copyright (I'd budget $2-5k for initial consultation + policy revision)

B) **Pivot to a more defensive model** (official embeds only, no thumbnail caching, permission-based featuring)

C) **Accept the risk** and build a war chest for legal defense (set aside 20% of any revenue)

**The site concept is great.** The execution is solid. But you're building on a copyright foundation that's more sand than concrete. Fix that before you scale.
